Back in 2015 the economist Simon Wren-Lewis coined the term mediamacro. The idea has its origins during the Great Recession, and the narrative coming from certain sections of the media that likened government spending with the spending of an individual household.

Language such as: "maxing out the government credit card", "living within its means" and “blowing the family budget", showed a misunderstanding of how many households operate (families who spend a lot more at Christmas for example) and a woeful misunderstanding of how macroeconomics work.

Simon's series of posts have stuck with me over the years. Firstly as a student of economics many years ago, but also because they are a great examination of how simple language supports epistemic capture. Establish a culturally resonant turn of phrase as a simple way of understanding a complex topic, and not only are you reinforcing your view of the world, you are shutting down other lines of thinking.

"No responsible person would max out the family credit card, so why is the government doing the same?!"

I encountered this rhetorical device when discussing assurance processes at NHS England. Allowing engineers to write their own tests was akin to “marking your own homework”. As someone who was new government and highly regulated environments I found myself nodding along.

Like a government with a credit card, the analogy makes sense at first but when you interrogate it a little deeper it gets quite problematic. Firstly it infantilises your teammates, likening them to a child with homework. It also suggesting that they might have an interest in "inflating their grade" without external oversight. Second, it creates an adversarial relationship and increases the likelihood of conflict. And in addition to being bad for morale, adversarial relationships are slow ones. Every sign-off, every check, every review is paid for in time.

Wren-Lewis was describing an period in which epistemic capture was strategy employed to push a particular policy, namely reducing the size of then state. By contrast, when people talk about "marking your own homework" with respect to assurance they aren't operating in bad faith. Yes, the turn of phrase might be a little disrespectful, but it represents a choice of process (assurance responsibility being external to the delivery team) that might have made sense once, but struggles to keep up with the pace of innovation today. And therein lies the tension. Modern multi-disciplinary teams know that in complex, fast-moving, socio-technical environments faster is safer.<a id="ref1" href="#ftn1"><sup>1</sup></a> And yet our assurance processes reflect a slower moving time when technical solutions were installed "on-prem" and were unlikely to be connected to the Internet.

Obviously the answer is not that we do away with assurance. The highest levels of clinical and technical safety should be demanded of an organisation with the reach and access to data that the NHS has. What's more the instinct behind keeping assurance external to delivery teams wasn't wrong either. The blind spots are real, and someone needs a vantage point different from the people building the thing. But that need doesn't require a separate team running a separate process. I wonder if the NHS MDT of the future includes those perspectives part of the team to begin and gives everyone the same incentive. One team with the goal of safe delivery, not two teams with one responsible for safety and the other for delivery.
