# Doing product in NHS England

My experience of working in product over the last twenty years has been that you fill whatever space is necessary in the hunt for value.

In the early phase of my career there was a lot of space to fill. The professional disciplines that make up digital teams were less well defined than they are today, and so as well as bringing people together, I often found myself doing things that became the domain of service designers, user researchers, data analysts, growth marketeers and probably others too.

As all these professions came closer together, particularly engineering and design, product got crowded out. They became better versed in what value meant and how to hunt it down. They were customer centred, focused on outcomes, and understood the maxim of ship small and often. What was left for PMs was the thread between disciplines, what Dan Schmitt called "[the white space](https://productlogic.org/2014/06/22/the-product-management-triangle/)".

## The question I couldn't answer

Thinking back through my startup years, I think it was possible to generalise what was meant by value. Some combination of positive outcomes for users and for the business, filtered through the resources at your disposal. The classic formula:

Impact (1→n) × Effort (0→1) = Value

Impact scales upward. Effort, scored as a fraction, dampens it.

I remember being asked what value meant during my first few months at NHS England and being absolutely floored by the question. It was during an all day workshop for one of our teams and we I’d been asked to drop in and say hi and so it caught me guard. I can’t remember exactly what I said, but I hope it was combination of: well this is how I used to think about it, but that's not the whole picture around here. It was probably far less eloquent.

What this slide unlocked for me was that the formula wasn't wrong. It was just incomplete. Value is still a function of impact and effort, but also of the political will to see your work from idea through to delivery. 

For a long time now Richard has argued that digital services are politics, and if that is true (I am sure it is) then is stands to reason that *the* politics surrounding your work is a critical hurdle to navigate.

## What this means for product

If PMs fill the white space between disciplines, then in NHS England — and other large, matrix-style organisations grappling with wicked problems (thanks Mike) — that space is politics. Engineering, Design and Delivery have, by and large got this, when it comes to assessing impact and understanding the resources at their disposal. PMs given their tendency to own communication of the vision, strategy and roadmap, need to take care of the politics.

If there is a playbook to follow here I have yet to read it. And much like assessing impact, assessing the politics it will be highly context dependent. Start with asking who gets to opine on your work?

- What leverage do they have over the team?
- What makes them tick? 
- What's their long term plan?
- Would they champion your idea? Stay neutral? Start to ruffle features?

Don't shy away from these ideas because the are political. To the extent that you can, bring what is political out into the open. Map it. Make it intelligible for your team. RAG rate it in your backlog if you fancy.

If you're not thinking about the politics, someone else is, and they may not share your priorities.
